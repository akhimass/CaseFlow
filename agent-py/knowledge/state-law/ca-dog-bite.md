California dog-bite primer. California imposes strict liability on dog owners: the owner is liable for a bite that occurs in a public place or while the victim is lawfully on private property, regardless of whether the dog had ever bitten before or shown viciousness (Civ. Code §3342). This is stronger for victims than the "one free bite" rule used in some states — the victim does not have to prove the owner knew the dog was dangerous. A documented prior history of aggression can still strengthen damages and defeat defenses, but it is not required for liability. The strict-liability statute covers bites specifically; injuries a dog causes another way (knocking someone down) fall under ordinary negligence. The filing window is two years from the bite (Code Civ. Proc. §335.1).
